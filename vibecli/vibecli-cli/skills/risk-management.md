# Risk Management

When working with risk management:

1. Apply structured risk identification techniques: brainstorming sessions, SWOT analysis, checklists from lessons learned, expert interviews, Delphi method, root cause analysis, assumption analysis, and process flow review. Cast a wide net across technical, operational, financial, legal, and reputational categories.

2. Build probability-times-impact risk matrices with clearly defined scales (e.g., 1-5 for both axes), consistent rating criteria, and color-coded heat maps (red/amber/green). Define thresholds that map to required response levels and escalation paths.

3. Distinguish between qualitative risk analysis (ranking risks by severity for prioritization) and quantitative risk analysis (numerical probability distributions, expected monetary value, decision trees). Use qualitative for initial triage and quantitative for high-priority risks requiring cost-benefit justification.

4. Define risk response strategies appropriate to each risk: avoid (eliminate the threat), transfer (insurance, contracts), mitigate (reduce probability or impact), accept (active with contingency or passive). For opportunities: exploit, share, enhance, or accept. Assign response owners and budgets.

5. Maintain a comprehensive risk register with unique ID, description, category, cause, trigger, probability, impact, risk score, response strategy, owner, status, review date, and residual risk rating. Keep it as a living document reviewed at regular intervals.

6. Apply Monte Carlo simulation for schedule and cost risk analysis by defining probability distributions (triangular, PERT, uniform) for uncertain variables, running thousands of iterations, and interpreting results as confidence levels (P50, P80, P90) for informed decision-making.

7. Implement Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) frameworks: COSO ERM for integrated governance alignment, ISO 31000 for principles-based risk management process, or sector-specific frameworks (Basel III for banking, Solvency II for insurance). Ensure top-down risk appetite cascades to operational risk tolerances.

8. Manage operational risks across people (key person dependency, skills gaps), processes (single points of failure, manual errors), systems (IT outages, cyber threats), and external events (supply chain disruption, regulatory change). Use KRIs for early warning and incident databases for trend analysis.

9. Develop Business Continuity Plans (BCP) with business impact analysis (BIA), recovery time objectives (RTO), recovery point objectives (RPO), crisis communication plans, alternate site procedures, and regular testing (tabletop exercises, functional drills, full-scale simulations).

10. Define and communicate risk appetite statements that articulate the level of risk the organization is willing to accept in pursuit of objectives. Translate appetite into measurable risk tolerance thresholds for each risk category and business unit.

11. Establish Key Risk Indicators (KRIs) as leading metrics that signal increasing risk exposure before events materialize. Define green/amber/red thresholds, assign monitoring ownership, set reporting frequency, and link KRI breaches to predefined escalation and response actions.

12. Produce risk reports tailored to audience: executive dashboards with top risks and trend arrows, board reports with strategic risk landscape and appetite utilization, operational reports with detailed mitigation progress, and project reports with updated risk registers and burn-down of open items.
