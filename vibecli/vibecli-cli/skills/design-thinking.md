# Design Thinking

When applying design thinking and user-centered design:

1. Empathize deeply through user interviews and contextual observation, focusing on what people do (not just what they say), uncovering latent needs, pain points, and workarounds they have invented.
2. Define the problem with a clear problem statement and How Might We (HMW) questions that reframe challenges as opportunities, narrowing scope without constraining solutions.
3. Ideate broadly using brainstorming, crazy 8s (eight sketches in eight minutes), and other divergent techniques, deferring judgment to maximize the volume and diversity of ideas.
4. Prototype rapidly with low-fidelity mockups (paper sketches, wireframes) before investing in clickable prototypes, making ideas tangible enough to provoke honest feedback.
5. Test with real users through usability testing and A/B experiments, focusing on observed behavior over stated preferences, and iterating quickly based on findings.
6. Create journey maps that visualize the end-to-end user experience across touchpoints, identifying moments of delight and frustration that reveal the highest-impact improvement areas.
7. Build personas grounded in actual research data rather than assumptions, capturing goals, behaviors, and contexts that drive meaningful design decisions.
8. Use affinity diagrams for synthesis by clustering interview notes, observations, and data points into themes, revealing patterns that inform problem definition and solution direction.
9. Run design sprints following the Google Ventures format (map, sketch, decide, prototype, test) to compress months of work into five focused days with clear outcomes.
10. Iterate based on feedback by treating every release as a learning opportunity, establishing tight feedback loops that inform the next cycle of empathize-define-ideate-prototype-test.
11. Embed accessibility in the design process from the start, designing for diverse abilities and contexts (low vision, motor impairment, cognitive load, low bandwidth) rather than retrofitting.
12. Measure design impact with metrics tied to user outcomes (task completion rate, time on task, error rate, satisfaction scores) and business outcomes (conversion, retention, support volume).
