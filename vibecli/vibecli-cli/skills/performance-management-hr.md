# Performance Management

When working with performance management and employee evaluation:

1. Establish goal-setting frameworks using SMART criteria (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) or OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) to align individual contributions with organizational strategy.
2. Shift from annual-only reviews to continuous feedback models with regular one-on-ones, quarterly check-ins, and real-time recognition to keep performance conversations timely and actionable.
3. Implement 360-degree feedback programs thoughtfully by training raters on constructive feedback, ensuring anonymity for peer and direct-report feedback, and coaching managers on interpreting multi-source data.
4. Conduct performance calibration sessions where managers discuss and align ratings across teams to ensure consistency, reduce bias, and establish shared standards for each performance level.
5. Design rating scales with clear behavioral anchors for each level; decide whether to use forced distribution or open-ended scales based on organizational culture and legal risk tolerance.
6. Structure Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs) with specific, measurable expectations, a reasonable timeline (typically 30-90 days), documented support resources, and clear consequences for non-improvement.
7. Develop high-performer retention strategies including stretch assignments, accelerated development paths, skip-level meetings with senior leadership, and differentiated compensation adjustments.
8. Document performance consistently throughout the cycle with written notes, examples of work, and feedback records; this protects the organization legally and provides richer review conversations.
9. Link performance outcomes to compensation decisions transparently, defining how ratings map to merit increases, bonus payouts, and equity refreshes while communicating the methodology to employees.
10. Train managers on delivering effective reviews: balancing positive and developmental feedback, using specific examples, practicing difficult conversations, and avoiding recency bias.
11. Guide employees in self-assessment preparation by encouraging them to track accomplishments, gather peer feedback, reflect on development areas, and propose goals for the next cycle.
12. Address legal considerations by ensuring performance documentation is factual and consistent, avoiding discriminatory language, applying standards uniformly, and consulting HR or legal counsel before termination decisions tied to performance.
