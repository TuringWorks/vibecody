# Career Development

When planning career growth and professional development:

1. Understand the IC vs management track: IC leadership scales through technical depth, architecture influence, and mentorship; management scales through people, process, and organizational impact. Switching between tracks is healthy and common.
2. Learn the engineering ladder levels and what is expected at each: junior (task execution), mid (feature ownership), senior (project leadership and mentorship), staff (cross-team technical direction), principal (org-wide strategy).
3. Maintain a brag document updated weekly with accomplishments, impact metrics, positive feedback, and challenges overcome; this is the raw material for promotion packets and performance reviews.
4. Seek stretch assignments that push you one level above your current scope: lead a cross-team project, own an on-call rotation, design an RFC, or mentor a junior engineer.
5. Find both mentors (who advise you) and sponsors (who advocate for you in rooms you are not in); mentors help you grow, sponsors help you get opportunities.
6. Build internal visibility through tech talks, RFC authorship, design document reviews, and cross-team collaboration; decision-makers need to see your impact firsthand.
7. Develop external visibility through blog posts, open source contributions, and conference talks; this builds your professional reputation and creates opportunities beyond your current role.
8. Conduct a regular skill gap analysis: compare your current skills against the next level's expectations, identify gaps, and create a focused learning plan with measurable milestones.
9. Prepare for performance reviews by gathering data (metrics, feedback, project outcomes) well in advance; frame accomplishments in terms of business impact, not just technical effort.
10. Network intentionally within and outside your company: attend cross-team meetings, join working groups, participate in industry communities, and maintain relationships with former colleagues.
11. Recognize when to move on: if you have stopped learning, your contributions are not valued, or growth opportunities have dried up, exploring new roles is a healthy career strategy.
12. Practice work-life integration by setting boundaries, managing energy (not just time), investing in recovery, and aligning your work with your personal values and long-term goals.
