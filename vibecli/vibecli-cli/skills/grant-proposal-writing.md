# Grant and Proposal Writing

When working with grant writing and proposal development:

1. Analyze the RFP or funding announcement thoroughly by building a compliance matrix that maps every requirement, evaluation criterion, and submission instruction to specific sections of your response, ensuring nothing is missed and every mandatory element is addressed.
2. Write a compelling executive summary that captures the evaluator's attention in the first paragraph: state the problem, your proposed solution, key differentiators, expected outcomes, and the requested funding amount or contract value in clear, confident language.
3. Articulate the problem statement with evidence: use data, statistics, and citations to establish the significance and urgency of the problem you are addressing, making the case that the status quo is unacceptable and that your approach is the right response.
4. Develop the methodology and approach section with sufficient detail to demonstrate feasibility: describe your technical approach, work breakdown structure, timeline with milestones, risk mitigation strategies, and how your methods connect logically to the stated objectives and outcomes.
5. Build a detailed budget justification that connects every line item to project activities: explain personnel costs with level of effort, equipment and supply needs, travel purposes, subcontractor roles, indirect cost rates, and cost-sharing or matching commitments where applicable.
6. Present team qualifications and past performance by highlighting relevant experience, domain expertise, key personnel bios with specific accomplishments, organizational capabilities, and references from similar successful projects that demonstrate your ability to deliver.
7. Align your narrative explicitly with evaluation criteria by using the same language and structure as the RFP, addressing each criterion in the order listed, and making it easy for evaluators to score your proposal favorably against their rubric.
8. Develop win themes and discriminators that thread throughout the proposal: identify 3-5 reasons the evaluator should select your team over competitors, and reinforce these themes consistently in the executive summary, technical approach, management plan, and past performance sections.
9. Implement a structured proposal review process: conduct a Pink Team review of the outline and compliance matrix, a Red Team review of the full draft for persuasiveness and compliance, and a Gold Team final review for executive sign-off, pricing validation, and production readiness.
10. Use graphics and visual storytelling strategically: include process flow diagrams, organizational charts, timelines, comparison tables, and annotated figures with action captions that communicate key messages even when skimming, following the principle that every graphic should have a clear takeaway.
11. Meet all compliance and formatting requirements precisely: page limits, font size and margins, file naming conventions, required forms and certifications, electronic submission procedures, and deadline adherence, as non-compliant proposals are often eliminated without evaluation.
12. Plan post-submission follow-up activities including responding to clarification requests promptly, preparing for oral presentations or site visits if applicable, debriefing on wins and losses to improve future proposals, and maintaining relationships with funding agencies or procurement offices for future opportunities.
