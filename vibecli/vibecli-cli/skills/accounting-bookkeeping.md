# Accounting and Bookkeeping

When working with accounting, bookkeeping, and financial management:

1. Design the chart of accounts by organizing accounts into the five fundamental categories (assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, expenses), using a logical numbering scheme that allows for future expansion (1000s for assets, 2000s for liabilities, 3000s for equity, 4000s for revenue, 5000s for expenses), creating sub-accounts for granular tracking without overcomplicating the structure, aligning account categories with your financial reporting needs, mapping accounts to tax return line items for efficient filing, and reviewing the chart annually to add, merge, or inactivate accounts as the business evolves.

2. Apply double-entry bookkeeping consistently by ensuring every transaction has equal debits and credits, understanding the normal balance for each account type (assets and expenses are debit-normal, liabilities, equity, and revenue are credit-normal), recording transactions at the time they occur following the accrual basis of accounting, posting journal entries with clear descriptions and supporting documentation, reconciling sub-ledgers to the general ledger regularly, and using adjusting entries at period-end to properly recognize revenue and expenses.

3. Manage accounts payable workflow by capturing invoices promptly through a centralized intake process (email, portal, or scan), matching invoices to purchase orders and receiving documents (three-way match), coding invoices to the correct expense accounts and cost centers, routing invoices through an approval workflow based on amount thresholds, scheduling payments to optimize cash flow while capturing early payment discounts, maintaining vendor master records with accurate payment terms and banking details, and reconciling the AP sub-ledger to the general ledger monthly.

4. Manage accounts receivable workflow by generating invoices promptly upon delivery of goods or services, establishing clear payment terms (Net 30, Net 60, due on receipt) communicated upfront, sending automated payment reminders before and after due dates, implementing a structured collections process with escalation stages, applying cash receipts accurately to outstanding invoices, tracking aging buckets (current, 30, 60, 90, 120+ days) and calculating the allowance for doubtful accounts, and reviewing credit limits and customer payment history periodically.

5. Perform bank reconciliation by comparing the bank statement balance to the general ledger cash account balance, identifying outstanding checks (issued but not yet cleared), recording deposits in transit (received but not yet credited by the bank), investigating and recording bank fees, interest income, and electronic transfers, matching cleared transactions individually to book entries, resolving discrepancies within the same accounting period, and documenting the reconciliation with sign-off for audit trail purposes.

6. Execute the month-end close process by following a standardized close checklist with assigned owners and due dates, recording all revenue and expense accruals for the period, posting depreciation and amortization entries, reconciling all balance sheet accounts (cash, receivables, payables, inventory, prepaids, fixed assets, debt), reviewing the trial balance for unusual balances or variances, preparing and reviewing financial statements (income statement, balance sheet, cash flow statement), and completing the close within a target number of business days.

7. Prepare financial statements by generating the income statement showing revenue, cost of goods sold, gross profit, operating expenses, and net income for the period, building the balance sheet presenting assets, liabilities, and equity as of the period end, constructing the cash flow statement reconciling net income to operating, investing, and financing cash flows, including notes and disclosures that explain significant accounting policies and unusual items, comparing results to budget and prior periods, and ensuring statements tie to each other (net income to retained earnings, ending cash to balance sheet).

8. Ensure GAAP and IFRS compliance by following the revenue recognition standard (ASC 606 / IFRS 15) with its five-step model, applying the lease accounting standard (ASC 842 / IFRS 16) for operating and finance leases, properly classifying current versus non-current assets and liabilities, using the correct inventory valuation method (FIFO, weighted average) consistently, assessing impairment of long-lived assets and goodwill when indicators exist, maintaining consistency in accounting policies and disclosing any changes, and staying current with new standards and their effective dates.

9. Handle tax preparation and planning by maintaining tax-ready books with proper account coding aligned to tax return categories, tracking temporary and permanent differences between book and tax income, calculating and recording the quarterly estimated tax provision, managing sales tax collection and remittance by jurisdiction, tracking depreciation using both book and tax methods (MACRS for US tax), planning for tax-efficient timing of deductions and income recognition, and organizing supporting documentation for efficient and audit-ready tax filing.

10. Set up and configure QuickBooks or Xero by selecting the right plan tier for your business needs, importing or creating the chart of accounts, connecting bank and credit card feeds for automatic transaction import, configuring invoice and estimate templates with company branding, setting up recurring transactions for regular expenses and invoices, establishing user roles and permissions with appropriate access levels, and integrating with payroll, inventory, and point-of-sale systems as needed.

11. Maintain audit trail and documentation by retaining source documents (invoices, receipts, contracts, bank statements) in an organized filing system, attaching supporting documentation to journal entries and transactions, implementing approval workflows that create a record of who authorized each transaction, using system-generated audit logs that track changes to accounting records, following document retention policies aligned with legal and regulatory requirements (typically seven years for tax-related documents), and preparing workpapers that link financial statement line items to supporting schedules.

12. Implement budgeting and variance analysis by building the annual budget through a collaborative process involving department managers, using historical trends as a baseline adjusted for known changes and strategic initiatives, breaking the annual budget into monthly or quarterly periods with seasonality, comparing actual results to budget each period and calculating variances (favorable and unfavorable), investigating material variances by identifying volume, price, and mix effects, presenting variance commentary to management with corrective action recommendations, and using rolling forecasts to update projections as conditions change throughout the year.
