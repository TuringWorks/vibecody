# Electrical Engineering & PCB Design

When working with circuit and PCB design:

1. Capture schematics with clarity and consistency — organize the schematic into logical sheets (power supply, microcontroller, interfaces, connectors) with a clear hierarchy, draw signal flow left-to-right and top-to-bottom, use net labels rather than long wires for inter-sheet connections, add decoupling capacitors near the pins they serve (not grouped in a separate section), include test points for critical signals, annotate components with meaningful reference designators (use consistent prefixes: R for resistors, C for capacitors, U for ICs), and add notes explaining non-obvious design choices directly on the schematic.

2. Select components with attention to availability and lifecycle — check distributor stock (DigiKey, Mouser, LCSC) for current availability and lead times before committing to a part, prefer components with multiple sources and JEDEC-standard footprints, verify that operating temperature range matches the application environment, check for end-of-life (EOL) or not-recommended-for-new-designs (NRFND) status, use manufacturer evaluation kits to prototype critical ICs before layout, maintain a preferred parts list for commonly used passives and connectors, and always verify the datasheet version matches your design assumptions.

3. Design PCB stackup and layout for signal and power integrity — define the layer stackup early based on signal speed and impedance requirements (use 4+ layers for anything with DDR, USB 3.x, or Ethernet), place ground planes adjacent to signal layers for controlled impedance, route high-speed differential pairs first with proper length matching, keep return current paths short and continuous (avoid splits in reference planes under high-speed traces), use via stitching around high-speed signals, follow manufacturer-recommended layout guidelines for ICs (especially BGA and QFN packages), and maintain minimum clearances per IPC-2221 or your design rules.

4. Run Design Rule Checks (DRC) rigorously and resolve all violations — configure DRC rules to match your PCB fabricator's capabilities (minimum trace width, spacing, drill size, annular ring), run DRC after every significant layout change rather than only at the end, treat all DRC errors as blockers (not warnings to ignore), verify copper-to-edge clearances meet fabrication requirements, check for acid traps and slivers that cause etching problems, validate solder mask and paste mask expansions, and cross-check the netlist against the schematic (ERC) to catch unconnected pins and shorted nets.

5. Ensure power integrity through proper decoupling and plane design — place bulk capacitors (10-100 uF) near power entry points and smaller capacitors (100 nF, 10 nF) as close as possible to IC power pins, use multiple vias for capacitor connections to reduce inductance, design uninterrupted power and ground planes (avoid routing signals through them), simulate power distribution network (PDN) impedance for high-speed designs, select decoupling capacitor values to cover the frequency range of interest (consider capacitor self-resonant frequency), and verify voltage regulator stability with the actual output capacitor ESR range.

6. Manage signal integrity for high-speed and mixed-signal designs — calculate and control trace impedance (50 ohm single-ended, 90-100 ohm differential are common targets), use impedance calculators or field solvers (Saturn PCB Toolkit, Si9000) with your actual stackup, terminate transmission lines appropriately (series, parallel, or AC termination based on topology), minimize stub lengths on high-speed buses, separate analog and digital ground regions with a single-point connection, guard sensitive analog traces with ground pours, and use simulation tools (HyperLynx, IBIS models) for critical signal paths.

7. Address thermal management in the design — calculate power dissipation for all active components and verify junction temperatures are within limits, use thermal vias under exposed pads (QFN, BGA) connected to internal copper planes, provide adequate copper area for power components (refer to IPC-2152 for current capacity), consider airflow direction when placing heat-generating components, add thermal relief on pads connected to large copper planes (to aid soldering), use thermal simulation tools for dense or high-power designs, and specify appropriate copper weight (1 oz standard, 2 oz for power, 0.5 oz for fine-pitch) per layer.

8. Design for EMI/EMC compliance from the start — minimize loop areas for high-frequency current paths (keep signal and return close together), use ground planes as shields and return current paths, filter all cables and connectors at board entry/exit points, place crystals and oscillators close to their ICs with ground guard rings, route clocks and high-speed signals on internal layers between ground planes, avoid routing high-speed signals near board edges, add common-mode chokes on differential interfaces (USB, Ethernet, HDMI), and plan for EMC testing (conducted and radiated emissions, immunity) per applicable standards (FCC Part 15, CISPR 32, IEC 61000).

9. Apply Design for Manufacturing (DFM) principles — use standard PCB panel sizes and avoid unusual board shapes when possible, maintain adequate spacing between components for automated pick-and-place (follow IPC-7351 for land patterns), orient polarized components consistently for inspection, avoid placing small components in the shadow of tall ones (reflow soldering issues), use fiducial markers for machine alignment, add tooling holes for fixturing, keep components away from board edges and V-score lines, design test pads accessible by bed-of-nails fixtures or flying probe testers, and consult your assembly house's DFM guidelines before finalizing the layout.

10. Generate correct manufacturing output files — export Gerber files (RS-274X format) for each copper, mask, and silkscreen layer, generate Excellon drill files with correct units and format, create an IPC-356 netlist for electrical testing, produce a pick-and-place centroid file with component coordinates and rotation, export a 3D STEP model for mechanical integration checks, include a fabrication drawing specifying stackup, materials, finish (HASL, ENIG, OSP), impedance requirements, and IPC class, and always verify Gerber output in a standalone viewer (GerberView, KiCad Gerber viewer) before sending to fabrication.

11. Maintain an accurate Bill of Materials (BOM) — include manufacturer part number, distributor part number, description, value, package/footprint, quantity, and reference designators for every component, flag any components with single-source risk, separate the BOM by assembly variant if the design supports multiple configurations, include DNP (Do Not Populate) components with clear notation, cross-reference BOM entries against schematic symbols to catch discrepancies, track component costs and calculate total BOM cost at target volumes, and version-control the BOM alongside the design files.

12. Prototype systematically and test with proper instrumentation — bring up power supplies first with current limiting before populating the full board, verify all supply voltages and ripple with an oscilloscope before powering active ICs, use a logic analyzer for digital bus debugging (SPI, I2C, UART), measure signal integrity on high-speed interfaces with proper probing technique (use ground springs, not long ground leads), perform boundary scan (JTAG) testing for BGA devices, run functional tests against the requirements specification, document all test results and any rework needed, and feed lessons learned back into the next revision of the design.
