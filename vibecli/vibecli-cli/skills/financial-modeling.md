# Financial Modeling

When working with financial models and valuation analysis:

1. Build a three-statement model as the foundation by linking the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement through a logical flow: net income flows to retained earnings on the balance sheet and to operating cash flow, capital expenditures from the cash flow statement drive fixed asset balances, debt issuance and repayment connect the balance sheet to financing activities, and changes in working capital accounts bridge accrual-based income to cash generation — always verify that the balance sheet balances and that the cash flow statement reconciles to the change in cash.

2. Construct a DCF valuation by projecting free cash flows (unlevered: EBIT after tax plus depreciation minus capex minus change in working capital) over a five to ten year explicit forecast period, selecting an appropriate discount rate using WACC (weighted average cost of capital derived from cost of equity via CAPM and after-tax cost of debt), calculating terminal value using either the perpetuity growth method or exit multiple approach, discounting all future cash flows to present value, performing a sensitivity analysis on the discount rate and terminal growth rate, and presenting the implied share price or enterprise value range.

3. Perform comparable company analysis by identifying a peer set of publicly traded companies with similar business models, size, growth profiles, and risk characteristics, calculating relevant trading multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, P/E, PEG ratio), normalizing for one-time items and accounting differences, computing the mean and median of the peer set, applying the range of multiples to the target company's metrics, and adjusting for company-specific premiums or discounts (size, growth, margin, market position).

4. Build an LBO (leveraged buyout) model by structuring the sources and uses of funds (equity contribution, senior debt, mezzanine, seller financing), constructing a detailed debt schedule with mandatory and optional prepayments, projecting free cash flow available for debt service, modeling management equity rollover and incentive plans, calculating returns at various exit years and multiples (IRR, MOIC), stress-testing the model under downside scenarios to ensure debt covenants are maintained, and presenting the waterfall of returns to each class of investor.

5. Forecast revenue rigorously by choosing the appropriate method for the business: bottom-up (units times price, customers times ARPU, same-store sales growth plus new stores), top-down (market size times market share), or a blend of both, incorporating seasonality and cyclicality patterns from historical data, benchmarking growth assumptions against industry trends and management guidance, modeling multiple revenue streams separately, and documenting every assumption with a clear data source or rationale.

6. Analyze cost structure by separating fixed costs from variable costs to understand operating leverage, modeling cost of goods sold as a percentage of revenue with improvement assumptions where justified, projecting operating expenses by category (R&D, sales and marketing, G&A) with clear drivers, incorporating step-function costs that change at capacity thresholds, benchmarking margin profiles against comparable companies, and identifying cost reduction opportunities while being realistic about implementation timelines.

7. Conduct sensitivity and scenario analysis by building data tables that show output metrics (NPV, IRR, share price) across ranges of key input assumptions, creating distinct scenarios (base case, bull case, bear case) with internally consistent sets of assumptions, using tornado charts to rank inputs by their impact on the output, implementing drop-down scenario selectors that dynamically update the model, stress-testing for extreme but plausible scenarios (recession, supply disruption, regulatory change), and presenting results as ranges rather than single-point estimates.

8. Evaluate capital budgeting decisions by calculating net present value (NPV) and accepting projects with positive NPV at the required hurdle rate, computing internal rate of return (IRR) while understanding its limitations with non-conventional cash flows, assessing payback period and discounted payback for liquidity-constrained decisions, considering profitability index (NPV per dollar invested) when choosing among mutually exclusive projects with different scales, incorporating real options value for projects with embedded flexibility, and aligning project evaluation with the company's strategic priorities.

9. Model working capital by forecasting accounts receivable using days sales outstanding (DSO), inventory using days inventory outstanding (DIO), accounts payable using days payable outstanding (DPO), analyzing historical trends and seasonal patterns in working capital ratios, calculating the cash conversion cycle (DSO plus DIO minus DPO), modeling the incremental working capital investment required to support revenue growth, and flagging when working capital changes significantly impact free cash flow projections.

10. Build the debt schedule and capital structure by modeling each tranche of debt separately (revolving credit facility, term loans, bonds, convertible notes), tracking drawn amounts, interest expense (fixed and floating), mandatory amortization, and optional prepayments from excess cash flow, implementing debt covenants (leverage ratio, interest coverage, fixed charge coverage) as checks in the model, modeling refinancing events and new issuances, calculating the blended cost of debt and WACC at each forecast period, and showing the deleveraging trajectory over time.

11. Audit models and check for errors by implementing a dedicated checks page that flags balance sheet imbalances, circular reference issues, and sign convention violations, using consistent color coding (blue for inputs, black for formulas, green for links), tracing precedents and dependents with Excel's audit tools, reviewing formulas for hardcoded values that should be dynamic, testing the model under extreme input values to ensure formulas behave correctly, having an independent reviewer walk through the model logic, and maintaining a change log for version control.

12. Prepare the presentation and executive summary by distilling the model output into a concise investment thesis or recommendation, creating a one-page summary with key metrics (valuation range, returns, key risks), building supporting exhibits (football field chart, sensitivity tables, bridge charts), tailoring the level of detail to the audience (board, investment committee, management), including a clear statement of key assumptions and risks, and ensuring that the narrative is consistent with the numbers and that the model file is clean and ready for due diligence review.
