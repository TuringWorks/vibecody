# Time Management

When working with time management and personal productivity:

1. Use the Eisenhower Matrix to categorize tasks by urgency and importance: Do (urgent + important), Schedule (not urgent + important), Delegate (urgent + not important), Eliminate (neither). Spend most energy in Quadrant 2 (Schedule) to prevent crises.
2. Follow GTD (Getting Things Done) methodology: capture everything into an inbox, clarify actionability, organize into projects/contexts/waiting-for lists, review weekly, and engage with confidence knowing nothing is forgotten.
3. Apply the Pomodoro Technique for focused execution: work in 25-minute sprints with 5-minute breaks, take a longer 15-30 minute break every four pomodoros, and track completed pomodoros to build awareness of effort.
4. Schedule deep work blocks (Cal Newport): dedicate 2-4 hour uninterrupted periods for cognitively demanding tasks, eliminate distractions (notifications, email, chat), and protect these blocks as non-negotiable appointments.
5. Batch similar tasks together to reduce context-switching overhead: group emails, phone calls, administrative tasks, and creative work into dedicated time blocks rather than interleaving them throughout the day.
6. Use calendar blocking and time boxing: assign every task a specific time slot on your calendar, set hard stop times for meetings and tasks, and treat your calendar as a commitment contract with yourself.
7. Say no strategically: evaluate requests against your priorities before accepting, use phrases like "I can do X but not until Y" or "That is outside my current focus," and remember that every yes is a no to something else.
8. Manage energy, not just time: identify your peak performance hours (chronotype), schedule high-cognitive tasks during peak energy, reserve low-energy periods for routine work, and protect recovery time.
9. Conduct weekly reviews and planning: review completed tasks and open loops every week, update project lists, identify the top 3 priorities for the coming week, and adjust plans based on what you learned.
10. Minimize context switching: group related tasks, use "do not disturb" modes, close unnecessary tabs and applications, and recognize that multitasking is a myth that reduces quality and increases completion time.
11. Manage interruptions proactively: establish office hours for availability, use visual signals (headphones, status indicators) to communicate focus time, batch responses to messages, and train colleagues on your availability patterns.
12. Set work-life boundaries and prioritize recovery: define clear start and end times for work, create transition rituals between work and personal time, take genuine vacations, and recognize that sustainable performance requires rest and renewal.
