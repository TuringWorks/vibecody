# Contract Law Basics

When working with contracts and legal agreements:

1. Ensure every contract contains the essential elements of formation: a clear offer, unambiguous acceptance, adequate consideration (something of value exchanged), capacity of the parties to contract, and lawful purpose.
2. Draft NDAs appropriate to the context: use mutual NDAs when both parties share confidential information and one-way NDAs when only one party discloses; define confidential information precisely, set a reasonable duration (typically 2-5 years), and specify permitted disclosures and exclusions.
3. Define SLAs with measurable commitments including uptime percentages (e.g., 99.9%), response and resolution times by severity level, penalty or credit mechanisms for breaches, escalation procedures, and exclusions for scheduled maintenance or force majeure events.
4. Understand software licensing models and their implications: perpetual licenses (one-time fee, ongoing maintenance), subscription licenses (recurring fee, access-dependent), open-source licenses (permissive vs copyleft obligations), and hybrid models with usage-based pricing.
5. Protect intellectual property through the appropriate mechanism: patents for novel inventions (utility or design), trademarks for brand identifiers (names, logos), copyrights for original works of authorship (code, content), and trade secrets for confidential business information with economic value.
6. Negotiate indemnification clauses carefully, understanding who bears liability for third-party claims; pair indemnification with limitation of liability caps (typically 1-2x annual contract value) and carve-outs for IP infringement, data breaches, and willful misconduct.
7. Include data processing agreements (DPAs) in contracts involving personal data, covering GDPR Article 28 requirements: processing purposes, data categories, sub-processor management, audit rights, breach notification timelines, and data return or deletion upon termination.
8. Review employment agreements for key provisions including at-will status or fixed term, non-compete scope and enforceability (varies significantly by jurisdiction), non-solicitation of clients and employees, invention assignment clauses, and confidentiality obligations that survive termination.
9. Draft termination and exit clauses that specify termination triggers (for cause, for convenience, material breach with cure period), notice requirements, transition assistance obligations, data portability, and survival of provisions like confidentiality and indemnification.
10. Choose dispute resolution mechanisms appropriate to the relationship: arbitration for speed and confidentiality (specify rules such as AAA or JAMS), mediation as a prerequisite step, or litigation with agreed jurisdiction and venue; consider the enforceability of each in relevant jurisdictions.
11. Manage the contract lifecycle from request through drafting, negotiation, approval, execution, obligation tracking, renewal or termination, and archival using a CLM system to maintain visibility and compliance across the portfolio.
12. Watch for red flags during contract review including unlimited liability, unilateral amendment rights, automatic renewal with long notice periods, broad IP assignment beyond project scope, most-favored-nation pricing without reciprocity, and non-mutual termination rights.
