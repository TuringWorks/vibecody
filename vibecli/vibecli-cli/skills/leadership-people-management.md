# Leadership and People Management

When leading engineering teams and managing people:

1. Run effective 1:1 meetings using three rotating formats: status (blockers, priorities), career (growth goals, aspirations), and coaching (skill development, challenges) to ensure both tactical and strategic needs are addressed weekly.
2. Delegate by providing context not control: share the why, define the outcome, set constraints, then let the engineer choose the how; avoid micromanaging implementation details.
3. Build psychological safety by normalizing failure, admitting your own mistakes publicly, responding to bad news with curiosity instead of blame, and rewarding people who surface problems early.
4. Give feedback using the SBI model (Situation-Behavior-Impact): describe the specific situation, the observable behavior, and the concrete impact it had; deliver praise publicly and constructive feedback privately within 48 hours.
5. Manage performance through continuous feedback rather than annual surprises; use Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs) only as a last resort after documented coaching conversations, clear expectations, and reasonable time to improve.
6. Plan team capacity by accounting for interrupts (typically 20-30% of time), on-call rotations, tech debt work, and leave; never plan sprints at 100% utilization.
7. Hire deliberately by defining a rubric before interviewing, using structured interviews with consistent questions, evaluating for both skills and values alignment, and involving the team in the process.
8. Set clear expectations by writing down roles, responsibilities, and success criteria; review them quarterly and adjust as the team and business evolve.
9. Manage up by understanding your leadership's priorities, framing requests in terms of business impact, providing concise status updates proactively, and never surprising your manager with bad news.
10. Create growth opportunities by assigning stretch projects, sponsoring engineers for visible cross-team work, supporting conference talks and open-source contributions, and building explicit promotion cases.
11. Handle underperformance early by having direct conversations, identifying root causes (skill gap, motivation, personal issues, unclear expectations), creating specific improvement plans with measurable milestones, and following up consistently.
12. Build team culture intentionally through shared rituals (demo days, tech talks, team retrospectives), celebrating wins, defining team values collaboratively, and modeling the behavior you want to see.
