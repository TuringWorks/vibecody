# Critical Thinking

When applying critical thinking and analytical problem solving:

1. Use first principles reasoning by breaking down problems to their fundamental truths, challenging inherited assumptions, and rebuilding understanding from the ground up rather than reasoning by analogy alone.
2. Apply the 5 Whys technique for root cause analysis, asking "why" iteratively until you move past symptoms to the underlying systemic cause, and verify the causal chain works in reverse.
3. Practice hypothesis-driven development by formulating explicit, falsifiable hypotheses before investigating, designing the smallest experiment that would confirm or refute each hypothesis.
4. Distinguish data from intuition by actively seeking empirical evidence to validate gut feelings, recognizing that intuition is useful for generating hypotheses but insufficient for confirming them.
5. Guard against cognitive biases: confirmation bias (seeking only supporting evidence), anchoring (over-weighting first information), and sunk cost fallacy (continuing due to past investment rather than future value).
6. Apply Occam's Razor by favoring the simplest explanation that accounts for all observed facts, adding complexity only when simpler models fail to explain the evidence.
7. Employ systems thinking to consider second-order and third-order effects, mapping feedback loops, unintended consequences, and emergent behaviors that arise from interconnected components.
8. Use the devil's advocate approach by deliberately arguing the opposing position, stress-testing ideas by actively seeking disconfirming evidence and weaknesses in the current reasoning.
9. Apply structured decision frameworks such as pros/cons matrices, weighted scoring models, or decision trees to make trade-offs explicit and reduce the influence of emotional reasoning.
10. Conduct pre-mortem analysis by imagining the project has failed and working backward to identify the most likely causes of failure, then proactively mitigating those risks.
11. Focus on asking the right questions before rushing to answers, distinguishing between symptoms and causes, and reframing problem statements to open up overlooked solution spaces.
12. Separate correlation from causation by demanding evidence of mechanism, considering confounding variables, and recognizing that two things trending together does not establish that one causes the other.
