# Brand Strategy

When working with brand strategy:

1. Define brand positioning by identifying a clear differentiation that competitors cannot easily replicate, articulating a value proposition that connects functional benefits to emotional outcomes, mapping the competitive landscape to find white space, distilling positioning into a concise statement (for [target audience], [brand] is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe]), and validating positioning with target customers before committing to creative execution.
2. Establish brand architecture by choosing the right model for the business: a branded house (single master brand like Google) for maximum brand equity transfer, a house of brands (distinct brands like Procter & Gamble) for diverse audiences, or an endorsed model (sub-brands backed by a parent like Marriott) for balance; define naming hierarchies, visual relationships, and the rules for when to create new brands versus extending existing ones.
3. Build a visual identity system that includes a primary logo with approved variations (icon, wordmark, lockup), a defined color palette with primary, secondary, and extended colors (with exact Pantone, CMYK, RGB, and HEX values), a typography system with heading and body typefaces and usage rules, an illustration and photography style guide, iconography standards, and layout principles including grid systems and spacing scales.
4. Develop brand voice and tone guidelines by defining core voice attributes (e.g., confident but not arrogant, warm but not casual), creating a voice chart with "we are / we are not" distinctions, providing writing examples for different contexts (marketing copy, support emails, social posts, error messages), specifying vocabulary preferences and words to avoid, and adjusting tone based on context (celebratory for wins, empathetic for problems, clear for instructions).
5. Create naming conventions and nomenclature by establishing rules for product and feature naming (descriptive vs invented vs metaphorical), maintaining a naming registry to prevent conflicts, testing names for international meaning and pronunciation, checking trademark availability early, defining how names relate to the brand architecture, and creating a glossary of approved terminology for consistent communication.
6. Conduct brand audits and competitive analysis by inventorying all brand touchpoints (website, packaging, signage, social, internal communications), evaluating consistency and quality across channels, mapping competitors on perceptual dimensions (price vs quality, innovation vs tradition), identifying gaps and opportunities, gathering stakeholder and customer perception data through surveys and interviews, and synthesizing findings into an actionable report with prioritized recommendations.
7. Craft brand storytelling by defining the brand's origin narrative (founding story), articulating the brand's purpose beyond profit (why the brand exists), developing a narrative framework (hero's journey with the customer as hero, brand as guide), creating signature stories that illustrate brand values in action, maintaining authenticity by grounding stories in real experiences, and adapting the core narrative for different channels and audiences.
8. Map customer perception by conducting quantitative brand awareness and association surveys, analyzing Net Promoter Score and brand sentiment over time, using social listening to track unstructured brand mentions, running focus groups for qualitative insight into emotional connections, building brand perception maps to visualize positioning versus competitors, and tracking perception shifts after campaigns or brand changes.
9. Measure brand equity through awareness metrics (unaided and aided recall), preference and consideration metrics (would you choose this brand over alternatives), loyalty metrics (repeat purchase rate, customer lifetime value), financial metrics (price premium the brand commands, brand valuation), and sentiment metrics (social sentiment score, review ratings); establish a brand health dashboard with quarterly reporting.
10. Execute a rebranding process by first establishing clear objectives (why the rebrand is necessary), conducting internal and external research, developing creative strategy and design explorations, testing concepts with target audiences, planning a phased rollout across all touchpoints (digital first, then physical), communicating the change internally before external launch, preparing a comprehensive asset migration plan, and allowing overlap periods for co-branding if transitioning gradually.
11. Align the brand internally by creating internal brand training programs, developing an employee brand handbook, establishing brand ambassador programs, integrating brand values into hiring and performance evaluation, hosting brand immersion sessions for new employees, providing easily accessible brand asset libraries and templates, and measuring internal brand alignment through periodic surveys; the brand must be lived internally before it can be authentic externally.
12. Govern brand assets and maintain consistency through a centralized digital asset management (DAM) system, clear approval workflows for branded materials, regular compliance reviews across business units and partners, co-branding and partner guidelines, a brand council or committee for strategic decisions, version-controlled templates for common materials, and scheduled brand guideline updates to keep the system relevant as the brand evolves.
