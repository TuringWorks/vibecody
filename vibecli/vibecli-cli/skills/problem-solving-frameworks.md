# Problem-Solving Frameworks

When working with structured problem solving and analytical thinking:

1. Apply the MECE principle (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) to break down any problem: ensure categories do not overlap and together cover all possibilities. This prevents double-counting and gaps in your analysis.
2. Build issue trees and hypothesis trees to decompose complex problems: start with the core question at the top, branch into sub-questions, and continue until you reach testable, actionable leaves. Hypothesis trees start with a proposed answer and identify what must be true.
3. Use the 80/20 principle (Pareto analysis) to focus effort: identify the 20% of causes driving 80% of the impact, prioritize those for investigation and action, and avoid spending equal time on all branches of your issue tree.
4. Apply standard consulting frameworks for business problems: profitability (revenue vs cost breakdown), market entry (market size, competition, capabilities, economics), M&A (standalone value, synergies, risks), and pricing (value-based, cost-plus, competitive).
5. Structure communications using Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR): establish the context (situation), identify what changed or what the challenge is (complication), and present your recommendation (resolution). This creates a compelling narrative arc.
6. Follow the Pyramid Principle for presenting recommendations: lead with the answer, group supporting arguments into logical clusters, order arguments by impact or logic, and support each argument with evidence. Audiences should understand your conclusion before the details.
7. Conduct structured brainstorming: define the problem clearly before ideating, use techniques like brainwriting or SCAMPER, separate idea generation from evaluation, build on others' ideas, and converge using prioritization matrices.
8. Use root cause analysis tools: 5 Whys for linear causation, fishbone (Ishikawa) diagrams for categorized causes, fault tree analysis for system failures, and Pareto charts to identify dominant causes from data.
9. Practice data-driven problem solving: define what data you need before collecting it, distinguish correlation from causation, use appropriate visualizations, sanity-check numbers with back-of-envelope calculations, and be transparent about data limitations.
10. Align stakeholders on the problem definition before solving: ensure all parties agree on what the problem is, what success looks like, what constraints exist, and who has decision authority. Misaligned problem definitions waste effort.
11. Iterate on solutions through rapid prototyping and feedback: propose a minimum viable solution, test it with stakeholders or data, refine based on feedback, and avoid over-engineering before validation. Perfect is the enemy of good enough.
12. Present recommendations with clarity and conviction: state your recommendation upfront, provide 2-3 supporting reasons with evidence, address risks and mitigations, specify next steps and owners, and anticipate objections with prepared responses.
