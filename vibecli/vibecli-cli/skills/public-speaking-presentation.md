# Public Speaking and Presentation

When preparing and delivering technical presentations:

1. Structure your talk with a clear arc — open with a hook (a surprising stat, a relatable problem, or a bold claim), define the problem, walk through your solution, and close with a concrete call to action.
2. Design slides for signal, not noise — use minimal text (six words per slide max), strong visuals, and one idea per slide; your slides support your words, they do not replace them.
3. Use storytelling techniques for technical content — frame architecture decisions as narratives ("we tried X, it failed because Y, so we built Z"); stories are remembered ten times longer than bullet points.
4. Handle Q&A with confidence — repeat the question for the audience, answer concisely, and say "I don't know, but I'll follow up" when appropriate; honesty builds more credibility than bluffing.
5. Manage nervousness through preparation — rehearse at least three full run-throughs out loud; anxiety comes from uncertainty, and preparation eliminates uncertainty; talent is secondary to practice.
6. Follow live demo best practices — always have a pre-recorded backup video, use large fonts and high-contrast themes, script each step, and test on the actual presentation hardware beforehand.
7. Engage the audience actively — ask questions, use a show of hands, include interactive polls, or pause for discussion; a monologue loses attention after eight minutes.
8. Master timing and pacing — allocate time per section, practice with a timer, and cut material ruthlessly; a focused 20-minute talk beats a rambling 45-minute one every time.
9. Rehearse strategically — practice in front of a colleague first, record yourself and watch it back, and rehearse transitions between sections since those are where speakers stumble most.
10. Nail the lightning talk format — for five-minute talks, pick exactly one idea, cut all context that is not essential, and end with a memorable one-liner; constraints breed clarity.
11. Record and review yourself — watch your own recordings to catch filler words, pacing issues, and body language habits; self-review accelerates improvement faster than any coaching.
12. Build a speaking portfolio — start with team brown bags, then internal all-hands, then local meetups, then conferences; each step builds confidence and material for the next.
