# Real Estate Basics

When working with real estate investment and property management:

1. Understand property valuation methods: comparable sales approach (what similar properties sold for recently), income approach (value based on net operating income and cap rate), and cost approach (land value plus replacement cost minus depreciation). Use the method most appropriate for the property type.
2. Analyze rental property financials rigorously: calculate cap rate (NOI divided by purchase price), cash-on-cash return (annual pre-tax cash flow divided by total cash invested), gross rent multiplier (price divided by annual rent), and ensure positive cash flow after all expenses including vacancy allowance.
3. Evaluate financing options carefully: conventional mortgages (20% down, best rates), FHA loans (3.5% down, PMI required), commercial loans (different underwriting criteria), and portfolio loans for non-conforming properties. Compare total cost of borrowing including fees, not just interest rates.
4. Conduct thorough due diligence before purchasing: order professional inspections (structural, roof, HVAC, plumbing, electrical), review title reports for liens and encumbrances, verify zoning and permitted uses, examine property tax history, review HOA documents if applicable, and assess environmental risks.
5. Follow property management best practices: screen tenants thoroughly (credit, income, references, background), maintain detailed records and separate bank accounts, respond to maintenance requests promptly, conduct regular property inspections, build relationships with reliable contractors, and comply with all landlord-tenant laws.
6. Draft comprehensive lease agreements: include rent amount and due date, security deposit terms, maintenance responsibilities, pet and subletting policies, lease term and renewal options, early termination clauses, rules and regulations, and ensure compliance with local fair housing laws and tenant protection ordinances.
7. Perform market analysis and location evaluation: study local employment trends, population growth, median incomes, school quality, crime rates, planned infrastructure projects, supply pipeline of new construction, and rent-to-price ratios. Location determines long-term appreciation and rental demand more than any other factor.
8. Leverage tax benefits of real estate: deduct mortgage interest and property taxes, use depreciation to shelter rental income (27.5 years residential, 39 years commercial), defer capital gains with 1031 exchanges, track all deductible expenses meticulously, and consult a CPA who specializes in real estate.
9. Compare commercial and residential investing: commercial properties (office, retail, industrial, multifamily 5+ units) offer higher returns but require more capital and expertise, use triple-net (NNN) leases to shift expenses to tenants, have longer vacancy periods, and are valued primarily on income rather than comparable sales.
10. Consider REIT investing for passive real estate exposure: Real Estate Investment Trusts provide diversification, liquidity, and professional management without direct property ownership. Evaluate REITs on funds from operations (FFO), dividend yield, portfolio quality, leverage ratios, and management track record.
11. Plan property maintenance and improvement ROI: budget 1-2% of property value annually for maintenance, prioritize improvements that increase rent or reduce vacancy (kitchens, bathrooms, curb appeal), avoid over-improving for the neighborhood, and track the return on every capital expenditure.
12. Develop exit strategies before you invest: know your timeline and options including holding long-term for cash flow, selling after forced appreciation (value-add renovation), 1031 exchanging into a larger property, refinancing to pull out equity, or converting to a different use. Every investment should have a clear exit plan.
