# Teaching and Training

When working with teaching, training delivery, and facilitated learning:

1. Apply adult learning principles (Knowles' andragogy): adults need to know why they are learning something, draw on their existing experience, prefer self-directed learning, want immediately applicable knowledge, and are motivated by internal factors (relevance, problem-solving) more than external ones.
2. Accommodate different learning styles and preferences: visual learners benefit from diagrams and demonstrations, auditory learners from discussion and explanation, reading/writing learners from documentation and notes, and kinesthetic learners from hands-on practice. Effective training blends all modalities.
3. Use engaging presentation techniques: start with a hook (story, question, surprising statistic), vary your delivery pace and tone, use visuals instead of text-heavy slides, incorporate audience interaction every 10-15 minutes, and close with a clear summary and call to action.
4. Manage the classroom or virtual room effectively: establish ground rules at the start, start and end on time, manage the energy level with breaks and activities, handle side conversations diplomatically, ensure equitable participation, and adapt your pace based on audience comprehension signals.
5. Employ questioning techniques strategically: use open-ended questions to stimulate thinking, apply the Socratic method to guide learners to discover answers themselves, wait at least 5-7 seconds after asking a question, build on responses to deepen understanding, and use questions to check comprehension.
6. Design hands-on exercises and labs: create exercises that mirror real-world tasks, provide clear instructions and success criteria, scaffold difficulty (start simple and build complexity), allow adequate time for completion, and debrief exercises to extract key lessons and address misconceptions.
7. Deliver feedback and assessment constructively: use the SBI model (Situation, Behavior, Impact) for specific feedback, balance positive reinforcement with developmental feedback, assess understanding through formative checks (quizzes, discussions, demonstrations) not just summative tests, and create psychological safety for making mistakes.
8. Adapt to the audience's level in real time: gauge prior knowledge at the start with a quick poll or assessment, adjust technical depth and pace accordingly, provide additional resources for advanced learners, offer extra support for beginners, and never make anyone feel inadequate for asking questions.
9. Use visual aids effectively: follow the assertion-evidence slide design (headline + visual evidence), limit text to key points, use diagrams and flowcharts for processes, employ progressive disclosure (reveal information step by step), and ensure all visuals are readable from the back of the room.
10. Manage difficult participants with tact: redirect the know-it-all by acknowledging their expertise and channeling it constructively, draw out the quiet participant with direct but gentle invitations, address the side-talker privately during breaks, and handle the challenger by validating their perspective before redirecting.
11. Create safe learning environments: establish that questions and mistakes are welcome, share your own learning journey including failures, use icebreakers to build rapport, avoid putting individuals on the spot without warning, and protect learners from judgment by peers.
12. Measure learning outcomes and continuously improve: use Kirkpatrick's four levels (reaction, learning, behavior, results) to evaluate training effectiveness, collect feedback immediately after and weeks later, track on-the-job application of skills, calculate ROI where possible, and iterate on content and delivery based on data.
