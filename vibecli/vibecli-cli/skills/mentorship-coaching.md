# Mentorship and Coaching

When developing engineering talent:

1. Understand the distinctions between mentor, coach, and sponsor — a mentor shares experience and advice, a coach asks questions to unlock the mentee's own answers, and a sponsor actively advocates for someone's advancement in rooms they are not in.
2. Apply the GROW model — establish the Goal (what do you want?), explore the Reality (where are you now?), brainstorm Options (what could you do?), and commit to the Will (what will you do and by when?).
3. Ask powerful questions instead of giving answers — "What have you tried so far?" and "What would success look like?" build problem-solving ability faster than handing someone a solution.
4. Set development goals with concrete timelines — vague aspirations like "get better at system design" become actionable as "lead the design review for the payments service by end of Q2."
5. Create pairing and shadowing opportunities — have junior engineers pair with seniors on production incidents, architecture reviews, and customer calls to accelerate learning through observation.
6. Provide stretch assignments intentionally — assign tasks slightly beyond current ability with a safety net; the gap between comfort and challenge is where growth happens.
7. Build personalized learning plans — combine books, courses, projects, and conversations tailored to the individual's goals and learning style rather than applying a one-size-fits-all curriculum.
8. Give actionable feedback using SBI (Situation, Behavior, Impact) — "In yesterday's code review (situation), you explained the trade-offs clearly (behavior), which helped the team make a faster decision (impact)."
9. Build confidence through small wins — sequence early tasks so the mentee experiences success, then gradually increase complexity; confidence compounds.
10. Facilitate knowledge sharing sessions — encourage mentees to present what they have learned in tech talks or brown bags; teaching is the fastest path to mastery.
11. Practice succession planning — actively develop people who can take over your responsibilities; your ability to grow others is the strongest signal of your own readiness for the next level.
12. Measure growth and impact — track observable progress (PRs reviewed, incidents handled, designs led) and gather 360 feedback quarterly to ensure development is on track.
