# Personal Finance

When working with personal finance and financial planning:

1. Choose a budgeting method that fits your lifestyle: the 50/30/20 rule (needs/wants/savings), zero-based budgeting (every dollar assigned a job), or the envelope system (cash or virtual envelopes for each category). The best budget is one you actually follow consistently.
2. Build an emergency fund of 3-6 months of essential expenses: keep it in a high-yield savings account for accessibility, build it gradually (start with one month), replenish it after any withdrawal, and consider a larger cushion if you have variable income or are the sole earner.
3. Manage debt strategically: use the avalanche method (highest interest rate first) to minimize total interest paid, or the snowball method (smallest balance first) for psychological momentum. Avoid taking on new high-interest debt while paying down existing balances.
4. Understand investing basics: invest in low-cost index funds for broad diversification, harness compound interest by starting early, maintain an asset allocation appropriate for your age and risk tolerance, avoid timing the market, and rebalance periodically.
5. Maximize retirement accounts: contribute enough to your 401(k) to capture the full employer match (it is free money), understand the tax advantages of traditional vs Roth contributions, know the annual contribution limits, and consider backdoor Roth strategies if income-eligible.
6. Optimize your tax situation: maximize pre-tax contributions (401k, HSA), harvest tax losses in taxable accounts, hold investments for over one year for long-term capital gains rates, use tax-advantaged accounts for high-growth assets, and consult a tax professional for complex situations.
7. Assess your insurance needs: maintain adequate health, auto, and renters/homeowners coverage, consider term life insurance if others depend on your income, review disability insurance (often undervalued), carry an umbrella policy for liability protection, and reassess coverage after major life changes.
8. Understand real estate fundamentals: buying is not always better than renting (run the numbers), factor in all costs (mortgage, taxes, insurance, maintenance, opportunity cost), save a 20% down payment to avoid PMI when feasible, and consider your expected tenure in the home.
9. Navigate equity compensation: understand vesting schedules, know the difference between RSUs (taxed at vesting), ISOs (favorable tax treatment with holding requirements), and NSOs (taxed as ordinary income at exercise), plan for tax events, and avoid over-concentration in your employer's stock.
10. Address estate planning basics: create a will and keep it updated, designate beneficiaries on all accounts, establish power of attorney and healthcare directives, consider a revocable living trust to avoid probate, and review your plan after major life events.
11. Set clear financial goals with timelines: define short-term (under 1 year), medium-term (1-5 years), and long-term (5+ years) goals, attach specific dollar amounts and dates, automate savings toward each goal, and track progress quarterly.
12. Avoid common financial mistakes: do not invest money you need in the short term, avoid lifestyle inflation that consumes every raise, do not chase past performance in investments, beware of high-fee financial products, do not co-sign loans lightly, and never invest in something you do not understand.
