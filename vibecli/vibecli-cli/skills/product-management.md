# Product Management

When managing product strategy, requirements, and delivery:

1. Conduct product discovery through customer interviews (ask about problems not solutions), usability testing, surveys, and data analysis; validate assumptions before committing engineering resources to build anything.
2. Write PRDs with clear structure: problem statement, target users, goals and non-goals, functional requirements, success metrics, technical constraints, and open questions; keep them living documents that evolve with learnings.
3. Plan roadmaps using the now/next/later framework: commit to near-term items with confidence, signal medium-term direction, and keep long-term items as themes; avoid date-driven roadmaps that create false precision.
4. Prioritize features using quantitative frameworks like RICE (Reach x Impact x Confidence / Effort), ICE (Impact x Confidence x Ease), or Kano (must-have, performance, delight) to make tradeoffs transparent and defensible.
5. Set OKRs that are ambitious but achievable: 3-5 objectives per quarter with 2-4 measurable key results each; track progress weekly, score honestly (0.7 is a good target), and use them for alignment not performance evaluation.
6. Define MVPs by identifying the smallest version that tests your riskiest assumption; ship it to real users, measure outcomes against success criteria, and iterate based on evidence rather than opinions.
7. Design A/B tests and experiments with clear hypotheses, measurable outcomes, sufficient sample sizes, and predetermined success criteria; avoid peeking at results early and document learnings regardless of outcome.
8. Perform competitive analysis by mapping the landscape on key dimensions (features, pricing, positioning), identifying gaps and opportunities, and monitoring changes quarterly; compete on unique value not feature parity.
9. Develop user personas based on research data not assumptions: include goals, pain points, behaviors, and context of use; use them to ground feature discussions and resolve design debates.
10. Create go-to-market strategies covering positioning, messaging, launch channels, sales enablement, documentation, and support readiness; coordinate across marketing, sales, and engineering for coherent launches.
11. Identify product-market fit signals through retention metrics (not just acquisition), Net Promoter Score, organic growth, willingness to pay, and qualitative feedback indicating the product solves a real problem.
12. Make data-driven decisions by defining key metrics upfront, building dashboards for visibility, combining quantitative data with qualitative insights, and being transparent about what the data does and does not tell you.
