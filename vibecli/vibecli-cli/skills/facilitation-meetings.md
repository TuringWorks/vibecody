# Facilitation and Meetings

When working with meeting facilitation and workshop design:

1. Clarify the meeting purpose before scheduling: every meeting should have one of four purposes — inform (status update), decide (choose a path), brainstorm (generate ideas), or align (build shared understanding). If you cannot articulate the purpose, do not hold the meeting.
2. Design agendas with time allocation: list each topic with an owner, time box, and expected outcome (decision, discussion, or FYI). Share the agenda at least 24 hours in advance and include any required pre-reads so participants arrive prepared.
3. Use structured facilitation techniques: round-robin ensures every voice is heard, dot voting efficiently prioritizes options, silent brainstorming (brainwriting) prevents anchoring bias, and 1-2-4-All scales individual thinking into group consensus.
4. Manage dominant participants constructively: redirect with "Let's hear from others," use structured turn-taking, privately coach repeat offenders, and design exercises that equalize participation such as written responses before discussion.
5. Apply clear decision-making frameworks in meetings: use DACI (Driver, Approver, Contributor, Informed) to clarify roles, distinguish between consent ("I can live with it") and consensus ("We all agree"), and explicitly state when a decision has been made and who owns execution.
6. Optimize virtual meeting practices: turn cameras on for engagement, use chat for parallel input, leverage breakout rooms for small-group work, mute when not speaking, record for absent members, and account for video fatigue by scheduling shorter sessions with breaks.
7. Design workshops with intentional phases: opening (context, goals, icebreaker), exploration (divergent activities, data gathering, idea generation), convergence (prioritization, decision-making), and closing (action items, next steps, feedback). Each phase needs a distinct facilitation approach.
8. Use visual facilitation and whiteboarding: capture ideas visually in real time using sticky notes, canvases, or digital whiteboards. Visual artifacts create shared understanding, make abstract concepts concrete, and serve as a record of the session.
9. Maintain a parking lot for off-topic items: when a valuable but off-topic idea surfaces, visibly capture it in a "parking lot" list, acknowledge the contributor, and commit to addressing it later. This keeps the meeting on track without dismissing contributions.
10. Ensure accountability with meeting notes and action tracking: document decisions, action items (with owners and deadlines), and open questions within the meeting. Distribute notes within 24 hours and follow up on action items in subsequent meetings.
11. Protect focus time with no-meeting days: establish team-wide or organization-wide meeting-free days (or half-days) to allow uninterrupted deep work. Consolidate meetings on specific days to create contiguous blocks of productive time.
12. Know when NOT to have a meeting: if the goal is purely information sharing, use email or a recorded video. If only two people need to talk, have a quick call instead of booking a group session. If there is no decision to make, cancel the meeting.
