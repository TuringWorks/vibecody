# Emotional Intelligence

When developing and applying emotional intelligence in engineering work:

1. Build self-awareness by recognizing your triggers — notice which situations (a dismissive code review, a last-minute scope change, being interrupted in meetings) provoke strong reactions; awareness is the first step to choosing a better response.
2. Practice self-regulation by responding instead of reacting — when you feel a surge of frustration or defensiveness, pause for three seconds before speaking; that gap between stimulus and response is where emotional intelligence lives.
3. Bring empathy to code reviews and feedback — remember that every pull request represents someone's effort and thought; lead with what works well, frame suggestions as questions ("have you considered...?"), and critique the code, never the person.
4. Read room dynamics in meetings — notice who has not spoken, who looks disengaged, who is talking over others; a simple "I'd like to hear from everyone" or "Alex, what's your take?" can transform meeting quality.
5. Manage stress and prevent burnout proactively — recognize early warning signs (cynicism, exhaustion, detachment) in yourself and others; sustainable pace is not a luxury, it is a prerequisite for quality engineering work.
6. Build trust through appropriate vulnerability — admitting "I don't know" or "I made a mistake" signals psychological safety to your team; leaders who model vulnerability give others permission to be honest.
7. Practice active listening with reflection and validation — instead of waiting for your turn to talk, reflect back what you heard ("It sounds like you're concerned about...") and validate the emotion ("That makes sense given the timeline pressure").
8. Adapt your communication style to your audience — some colleagues prefer direct, concise messages while others need context and warmth; observe what works for each person and adjust without losing authenticity.
9. Handle criticism constructively — separate the signal from the delivery; even poorly delivered feedback often contains useful information; thank the person, extract the actionable insight, and discard the noise.
10. Recognize team morale signals early — watch for declining participation in discussions, increasing sick days, shorter standup updates, or loss of humor; these are leading indicators that something needs attention before it becomes a crisis.
11. Support colleagues through challenges — when a teammate is struggling with a difficult bug, a personal issue, or imposter syndrome, sometimes the most valuable thing is not a solution but presence; ask "how can I help?" and mean it.
12. Model healthy work boundaries — log off at a reasonable hour, do not send weekend messages expecting replies, take your vacation days, and talk openly about it; your behavior sets the norm more powerfully than any policy.
