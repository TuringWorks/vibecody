# Conflict Resolution

When handling conflicts in engineering teams:

1. Practice active listening before responding — let each party fully express their perspective without interruption, paraphrase what you heard, and confirm understanding before offering any input.
2. Separate people from problems — focus on the technical or process issue at hand rather than attributing blame to individuals; use "the build is broken" not "you broke the build."
3. Use interest-based negotiation instead of positional bargaining — ask "what outcome do you need?" rather than debating fixed positions; underlying interests often reveal compatible solutions.
4. Apply de-escalation techniques early — lower your voice, slow the pace, acknowledge emotions ("I can see this is frustrating"), and suggest a short break if tensions rise.
5. Find common ground as a foundation — identify shared goals (shipping quality software, reducing on-call burden) and build outward from points of agreement.
6. Document agreements explicitly — write down what was decided, who owns each action item, and deadlines; shared documents prevent "I thought we agreed on X" disputes later.
7. Follow up on resolution within one to two weeks — check that agreements are being honored, ask if new friction has emerged, and adjust the plan if needed.
8. Know when to escalate — if direct conversation fails after two good-faith attempts, involve a manager or neutral third party rather than letting resentment fester.
9. Practice cultural sensitivity in conflicts — communication norms around directness, hierarchy, and saving face vary across cultures; ask about preferences rather than assuming.
10. Manage ego and emotions — recognize when your own defensiveness is driving the conversation; take a step back, breathe, and re-engage with curiosity instead of certainty.
11. Create safe spaces for disagreement — establish team norms that explicitly welcome dissent, such as designated devil's advocate roles or anonymous feedback channels.
12. Turn conflict into constructive debate — reframe disagreements as design reviews or trade-off analyses; channel the energy into better technical decisions with documented pros and cons.
