# Writing and Professional Communication

When working with business writing and professional communication:

1. Apply the BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) principle: state your key message, request, or recommendation in the first sentence or paragraph. Busy readers may only read the opening, so front-load the most important information.
2. Follow email etiquette best practices: write specific subject lines that indicate action needed, keep emails under 5 sentences when possible, use bullet points for multiple items, clearly state the ask and deadline, and distinguish To (action required) from CC (FYI only).
3. Use the Minto Pyramid Principle for structured writing: start with the governing thought (answer), group supporting ideas into logically ordered clusters (typically 3), support each idea with evidence, and use vertical and horizontal logic to verify coherence.
4. Master memo and one-pager formats: include a clear title and date, executive summary (2-3 sentences), context/background, analysis with supporting data, recommendation, and next steps. One-pagers force clarity and are preferred by senior leaders.
5. Write effective executive summaries: distill the entire document into one paragraph, cover the situation, findings, recommendation, and expected impact, write it last but place it first, and ensure it stands alone without requiring the reader to continue.
6. Prefer active voice and concise sentences: replace "The report was completed by the team" with "The team completed the report," eliminate filler words (very, really, basically, actually), and aim for an average sentence length of 15-20 words.
7. Analyze your audience before writing: consider their knowledge level, priorities, concerns, and preferred format. Adjust technical depth, tone, and length accordingly. Write for the decision-maker, not for yourself.
8. Integrate data visualization in documents: use charts and graphs instead of tables of numbers, choose the right chart type (bar for comparison, line for trends, pie only for parts of a whole), label clearly, and highlight the key takeaway in a title.
9. Follow a proofreading and editing checklist: check for clarity of main message, logical flow, grammar and spelling, consistent formatting, appropriate tone, accurate data, and correct recipient list. Read aloud to catch awkward phrasing.
10. Write effective meeting minutes and action items: capture decisions made (not full discussion), record action items with owners and deadlines, note parking lot items, distribute within 24 hours, and use a consistent template.
11. Practice Slack and Teams etiquette: use threads to keep channels organized, avoid unnecessary @channel and @here mentions, set status to indicate availability, use emoji reactions to acknowledge without cluttering, and move complex discussions to calls.
12. Apply cross-cultural communication sensitivity: be aware of high-context vs low-context communication styles, avoid idioms and slang in international settings, consider time zones in scheduling, respect naming conventions, and adapt directness to cultural norms.
