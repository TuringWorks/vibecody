# Instructional Design

When working with instructional design and course development:

1. Write measurable learning objectives using Bloom's taxonomy action verbs organized by cognitive level: Remember (list, define), Understand (explain, summarize), Apply (demonstrate, implement), Analyze (compare, differentiate), Evaluate (critique, justify), and Create (design, construct).
2. Follow the ADDIE model for structured course development: Analysis (learner needs, constraints, existing knowledge), Design (objectives, sequencing, assessment strategy), Development (content creation, media production), Implementation (delivery, facilitation), and Evaluation (formative and summative review).
3. Apply backward design (Understanding by Design) by starting with desired results and evidence of understanding before planning learning activities; this ensures assessments directly measure stated objectives and activities build toward those assessments.
4. Develop learner personas that capture the target audience's prior knowledge, motivations, learning preferences, technical proficiency, time constraints, and workplace context to inform design decisions and content tone.
5. Chunk and sequence content logically by grouping related concepts into manageable modules (following Miller's 7 plus-or-minus 2 principle), sequencing from simple to complex, and providing advance organizers that connect new material to existing knowledge.
6. Design assessments at multiple levels: formative assessments (knowledge checks, polls, practice exercises) throughout learning to gauge understanding, summative assessments (exams, projects, presentations) at completion to certify competency, and authentic assessments that mirror real-world task performance.
7. Apply Mayer's multimedia learning principles: use words and graphics together (multimedia principle), place text near corresponding graphics (contiguity), eliminate extraneous content (coherence), use conversational tone (personalization), and avoid redundant on-screen text with narration (redundancy).
8. Design for accessibility using Universal Design for Learning (UDL) by providing multiple means of engagement (choice, relevance), representation (text, audio, visual), and action and expression (varied assessment formats), while meeting WCAG standards for digital content.
9. Create storyboards for eLearning that specify screen-by-screen layouts, on-screen text, narration scripts, visual descriptions, interaction mechanics, branching logic, and feedback text before moving to development in authoring tools.
10. Incorporate gamification elements purposefully: points and badges for motivation, leaderboards for social engagement, branching scenarios for decision-making practice, levels for progressive challenge, and narrative framing for contextual meaning, always tied to learning objectives rather than added as decoration.
11. Build effective feedback mechanisms that are timely (immediate for practice, prompt for assignments), specific (referencing the exact error or success), constructive (explaining why and suggesting next steps), and differentiated (adaptive feedback paths based on the type of mistake).
12. Evaluate and iterate on course effectiveness by collecting learner feedback, analyzing completion and assessment data, observing facilitated sessions, conducting post-training performance reviews, and using findings to revise content, pacing, and activities in subsequent iterations.
