# Mechanical Engineering & CAE Analysis

When working with mechanical engineering and computer-aided engineering:

1. Apply Finite Element Analysis (FEA) methodology rigorously — start by clearly defining the analysis objective (static strength, fatigue life, buckling, modal frequencies), simplify the CAD geometry by removing small features (fillets, chamfers, holes) that do not affect results but increase mesh complexity, select the appropriate element type (shell for thin structures, solid for bulk components, beam for slender members), perform mesh convergence studies by refining the mesh until results change less than 5% between iterations, apply boundary conditions that accurately represent real-world constraints (avoid over-constraining), and always validate FEA results against hand calculations or known analytical solutions for similar geometries.

2. Set up Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations with care — define the computational domain large enough to avoid boundary effects on the region of interest (typically 5-10 characteristic lengths upstream and 10-20 downstream), select the appropriate turbulence model (k-epsilon for general industrial flows, k-omega SST for boundary layer accuracy, LES for transient vortex-dominated flows), generate a high-quality mesh with boundary layer refinement (y+ values matching the wall treatment method), set appropriate inlet/outlet boundary conditions based on known experimental data, run grid independence studies, monitor residuals and key quantities for convergence, and compare results against experimental data or correlations whenever possible.

3. Select materials based on application requirements and constraints — consider mechanical properties (yield strength, ultimate tensile strength, fatigue endurance limit, hardness, impact toughness), thermal properties (conductivity, expansion coefficient, maximum service temperature), corrosion and environmental resistance, weight constraints, cost and availability, manufacturability (machinability, weldability, formability), and regulatory requirements (food contact, biocompatibility, flame resistance). Use Ashby material selection charts to compare material families, and consult material databases (MatWeb, CES EduPack) for property data. Always use minimum guaranteed properties from material specifications (e.g., ASTM, SAE) rather than typical values.

4. Perform stress and fatigue analysis to ensure structural reliability — calculate static safety factors against yield (for ductile materials) and fracture (for brittle materials), apply appropriate failure theories (von Mises for ductile, maximum principal stress for brittle, Mohr-Coulomb for rock and concrete), use stress concentration factors (Kt) from Peterson's for notches, fillets, and holes, evaluate fatigue life using S-N curves with corrections for surface finish, size, reliability, and temperature (Marin factors), apply Goodman or Gerber diagrams for combined mean and alternating stress, consider fracture mechanics (stress intensity factor, crack growth rate) for damage-tolerant design, and use rainflow counting for variable-amplitude loading spectra.

5. Conduct thermal analysis for components and systems — identify all heat sources and sinks (power dissipation, ambient conditions, radiation surfaces), model conduction through solids using appropriate thermal conductivity values, account for convection with proper heat transfer coefficients (natural vs. forced convection, laminar vs. turbulent), include radiation for high-temperature applications (emissivity, view factors), perform steady-state analysis first to understand thermal distribution, then transient analysis for thermal cycling and startup/shutdown scenarios, evaluate thermal stresses from differential expansion between mating materials, and validate thermal models against thermocouple measurements or thermal imaging data.

6. Apply tolerance analysis and Geometric Dimensioning & Tolerancing (GD&T) — use GD&T per ASME Y14.5 to define functional requirements (flatness, parallelism, position, runout) rather than relying solely on bilateral dimensional tolerances, perform worst-case tolerance stackup analysis for critical assemblies, use statistical (RSS) tolerance analysis when justified by production volumes and process capability, specify datum features based on functional assembly requirements, ensure tolerance values are achievable by the intended manufacturing process (consult process capability tables for machining, casting, injection molding), and use tolerance analysis software (3DCS, CETOL) for complex multi-part assemblies.

7. Design for manufacturability across production methods — for machining: minimize setups, avoid deep pockets and thin walls, use standard tool sizes, specify appropriate surface finishes only where functionally needed; for casting: maintain uniform wall thickness, add draft angles (1-3 degrees), design generous fillets at intersections, consider shrinkage and parting line locations; for injection molding: maintain uniform wall thickness (2-4 mm typical), add draft (0.5-2 degrees per side), avoid undercuts or design sliding cores, place gates at thick sections; for sheet metal: use standard bend radii (minimum 1x material thickness), maintain minimum flange lengths, account for bend deduction and K-factor; and for welding: specify joint preparation and welding process, provide adequate access for the welder, and consider distortion.

8. Leverage rapid prototyping methods for design validation — use FDM/FFF 3D printing for quick concept models and fit checks (ABS, PLA, PETG), SLA/DLP for high-detail and smooth-surface prototypes, SLS for functional parts with good mechanical properties (nylon, glass-filled nylon), metal AM (DMLS, SLM) for complex geometries impossible to machine, CNC machining of prototypes from production-intent materials for mechanical testing, and silicone molding with urethane casting for low-volume production-quality parts. Always consider the prototype's purpose (visual, fit check, functional test, material validation) when selecting the method, and account for anisotropic properties in additively manufactured parts.

9. Conduct Design Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (DFMEA) — assemble a cross-functional team (design, manufacturing, test, quality, field service), identify all potential failure modes for each component and interface, assess severity (impact on safety and function), occurrence (likelihood based on design and materials), and detection (ability of current controls to catch the failure before it reaches the customer), calculate Risk Priority Number (RPN = S x O x D), prioritize actions for high-RPN items and any safety-critical failures regardless of RPN, define recommended actions with responsible owners and target dates, re-evaluate RPN after implementing corrective actions, and maintain the DFMEA as a living document throughout the product lifecycle.

10. Follow a structured CAE workflow for reliable results — pre-processing: import and clean CAD geometry, define material properties and element types, generate mesh with quality checks (aspect ratio, Jacobian, skewness), apply loads and boundary conditions with clear documentation of assumptions; solving: select appropriate solver and analysis type (linear/nonlinear, static/dynamic, implicit/explicit), set convergence criteria and time stepping, monitor solution progress for divergence or instability; post-processing: extract and visualize results (stress contours, deformation, flow fields), check for physically reasonable results (no stress singularities at boundary conditions, conservation of mass/energy in CFD), extract quantitative data at specific locations, and generate analysis reports with clear summary of assumptions, results, and conclusions.

11. Validate analysis results through correlation with physical testing — design test specimens and fixtures that replicate the loading and boundary conditions assumed in the analysis, instrument with strain gauges at high-stress locations predicted by FEA, use accelerometers for modal and vibration testing, apply loads using calibrated test equipment (hydraulic actuators, shaker tables), compare measured vs. predicted results quantitatively (percentage error at specific locations), investigate discrepancies systematically (check material properties, boundary conditions, mesh quality), update the analysis model based on correlation results, and document the validated model for future use.

12. Ensure compliance with applicable standards and codes — identify relevant design codes early in the project (ASME BPVC for pressure vessels, ASME B31 for piping, ISO 12100 for machinery safety, IEC 60204 for electrical safety of machines, NFPA 70 for electrical, EN 13445 for unfired pressure vessels in Europe), understand the specific requirements for stress allowables, safety factors, testing, and documentation, use code-approved materials and design methods, perform required calculations and document per code format, engage third-party inspection bodies (Notified Bodies, Authorized Inspection Agencies) when required, and maintain a compliance checklist throughout the design process.
