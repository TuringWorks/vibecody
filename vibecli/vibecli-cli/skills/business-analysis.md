# Business Analysis

When performing business analysis and requirements gathering:

1. Conduct stakeholder interviews and facilitated workshops to elicit requirements, using open-ended questions to uncover the true business need behind stated requests and surface conflicting priorities early.
2. Write business requirements documents (BRDs) that clearly separate business objectives, scope, functional requirements, and constraints, using unambiguous language and measurable success criteria.
3. Create process maps using BPMN notation or swimlane diagrams to visualize current-state (as-is) and future-state (to-be) workflows, making handoffs, decision points, and bottlenecks visible.
4. Develop use cases and user stories that capture actor-system interactions, writing user stories in the format "As a [role], I want [capability], so that [benefit]" with clear acceptance criteria.
5. Perform gap analysis comparing current state to desired state, categorizing gaps by severity and effort, and prioritizing closures that deliver the highest business value.
6. Conduct feasibility studies across technical, financial, and operational dimensions, assessing whether proposed solutions are buildable, affordable, and sustainable within organizational constraints.
7. Define acceptance criteria using the Given-When-Then format or explicit conditions of satisfaction, ensuring every requirement has a testable, unambiguous definition of done.
8. Maintain a traceability matrix linking business requirements to functional specifications, design artifacts, test cases, and deliverables to ensure complete coverage and change impact visibility.
9. Construct data flow diagrams (DFDs) to model how information moves through a system, identifying data sources, transformations, stores, and destinations at progressive levels of detail.
10. Capture non-functional requirements explicitly, covering performance (response time, throughput), scalability, security, reliability, maintainability, and compliance constraints with quantifiable thresholds.
11. Perform impact analysis for proposed changes by tracing affected requirements, processes, systems, and stakeholders, estimating effort and risk before approving change requests.
12. Build a business case that quantifies the expected return on investment, total cost of ownership, payback period, and risk profile, providing decision-makers with the evidence needed to approve or reject initiatives.
