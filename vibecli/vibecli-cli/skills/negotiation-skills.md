# Negotiation Skills

When negotiating in professional settings:

1. Know your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) — before entering any negotiation, clearly define what you will do if no deal is reached; your BATNA is your source of power and your walk-away point.
2. Prepare and research thoroughly — gather market data, understand the other party's constraints and motivations, and rehearse your key points; eighty percent of negotiation success comes from preparation done before the conversation starts.
3. Practice interest-based bargaining for win-win outcomes — instead of fighting over fixed positions ("I want X, you want Y"), explore underlying interests ("I need flexibility, you need predictability") to find creative solutions that satisfy both sides.
4. Understand the anchoring effect and first offers — the first number on the table disproportionately shapes the final outcome; when you have strong data, anchor first; when you lack information, let the other party anchor and adjust from there.
5. Use active listening as a negotiation tool — paraphrase the other party's concerns back to them before responding; people become more flexible when they feel genuinely heard and understood.
6. Manage your emotions under pressure — recognize when frustration or excitement is driving your decisions; take a pause, ask for a break, or say "let me think about that" rather than reacting in the moment.
7. Know when to walk away — if the deal falls below your BATNA or violates your core principles, leave the table gracefully; a bad agreement is worse than no agreement, and willingness to walk away strengthens your position.
8. Put agreements in writing and follow through — verbal agreements are forgotten or reinterpreted; document decisions, responsibilities, and timelines immediately; then deliver on every commitment to build a reputation for reliability.
9. Navigate multi-party negotiations strategically — identify allies, understand each party's priorities, build coalitions around shared interests, and address the most resistant party last after building momentum with others.
10. Respect cultural differences in negotiation — directness, pace, formality, and relationship-building expectations vary widely across cultures; research norms beforehand and ask when unsure rather than assuming your style is universal.
11. Build long-term relationships over short-term wins — optimize for repeated interactions; leaving value on the table today to maintain trust pays dividends across years of collaboration.
12. Apply negotiation to technical decisions through the RFC process — frame competing technical approaches as trade-off analyses rather than winner-take-all debates; document alternatives, evaluate against shared criteria, and let data drive the decision.
