# Vendor Management

When evaluating, contracting, and managing third-party vendors:

1. Evaluate vendors against weighted criteria covering cost, quality, reliability, security posture, scalability, support responsiveness, and cultural fit; use a scoring matrix to make comparisons objective and document the rationale for final selection.
2. Define SLAs with specific, measurable targets for uptime (e.g., 99.9%), response time, resolution time, throughput, and error rates; include clear measurement methods, reporting cadence, and consequences for non-compliance such as service credits.
3. Negotiate contracts with attention to key terms: pricing model (per-seat, usage-based, flat fee), payment terms, auto-renewal clauses, termination rights, data ownership, IP rights, liability caps, indemnification, and most-favored-nation pricing provisions.
4. Assess vendor risk across dimensions including financial stability, security practices, regulatory compliance, geographic and geopolitical exposure, key-person dependencies, and business continuity plans; reassess annually or when material changes occur.
5. Maintain performance scorecards that track SLA adherence, support quality, innovation and roadmap alignment, relationship health, and cost trends; review scorecards quarterly in joint business reviews with the vendor.
6. Establish escalation procedures with named contacts at each tier, defined response times, and clear criteria for when to escalate; ensure both sides understand the process and test it periodically.
7. Adopt a multi-vendor strategy for critical capabilities to avoid lock-in; maintain abstraction layers in your architecture, ensure data portability through standard formats, and keep alternative vendor evaluations current.
8. Manage licenses and compliance by maintaining a centralized inventory of all vendor agreements, tracking usage against entitlements, setting renewal reminders 90 days in advance, and auditing compliance annually to avoid surprise true-up costs.
9. Require vendor security reviews including SOC 2 Type II reports, ISO 27001 certification, penetration test results, and data processing agreements; for critical vendors, conduct on-site assessments and include security requirements in contracts.
10. Plan renewals strategically by starting evaluation 6 months before expiry, benchmarking current pricing against market rates, documenting pain points for negotiation leverage, and having a credible alternative ready to strengthen your position.
11. Develop exit strategies for every critical vendor: document data export procedures, map integration touchpoints, estimate migration effort and cost, maintain skills to operate alternatives, and include contractual provisions for transition assistance.
12. Invest in relationship management by assigning a dedicated vendor manager for strategic partners, scheduling regular executive check-ins, sharing your roadmap to enable proactive alignment, and treating the relationship as a partnership rather than a transaction.
