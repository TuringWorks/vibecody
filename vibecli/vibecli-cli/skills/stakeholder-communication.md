# Stakeholder Communication

When communicating with stakeholders across the organization:

1. Use a RACI matrix (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) to clarify roles for every major initiative; publish it early, get agreement from all parties, and reference it when confusion arises about who owns what.
2. Write executive summaries using BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front): lead with the conclusion or ask, then provide supporting context; executives should understand the key message in the first two sentences without reading further.
3. Report status using RAG (Red/Amber/Green) indicators with clear definitions: Green means on track, Amber means at risk with a mitigation plan, Red means off track and needs intervention; never hide a Red behind Amber.
4. Manage expectations proactively by communicating scope, timeline, and resource constraints early; when things change, notify stakeholders immediately with the impact and proposed alternatives rather than waiting for the next scheduled update.
5. Tailor your message to the audience: technical stakeholders want architecture and tradeoffs, business stakeholders want impact and timelines, executives want risks and decisions needed; never present the same deck to every audience.
6. Navigate difficult conversations using frameworks like Radical Candor (care personally, challenge directly) or Nonviolent Communication (observation, feeling, need, request); prepare key points in advance and focus on shared goals.
7. Write clearly and concisely in all written communication: use short paragraphs, bullet points, bold key information, eliminate jargon for non-technical audiences, and always include a clear call to action or next steps.
8. Facilitate meetings effectively by publishing an agenda in advance, starting with the decision or question to be answered, timebox discussions, capture action items with owners and deadlines, and send notes within 24 hours.
9. Document decisions using Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) or similar formats: capture the context, options considered, decision made, rationale, and consequences; make these searchable and link them from relevant project documents.
10. Define escalation paths clearly so team members know when and how to escalate: what constitutes a blocker, who to contact first, expected response times, and what information to include; remove ambiguity to speed resolution.
11. Track and visualize progress using dashboards, burndown charts, or milestone trackers that are accessible to all stakeholders; update them regularly and proactively highlight deviations from plan before stakeholders have to ask.
12. Build trust through transparency by sharing both good and bad news promptly, admitting what you do not know, following through on commitments, and being consistent in your communication cadence and quality over time.
