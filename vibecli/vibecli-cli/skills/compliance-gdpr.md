# GDPR and Data Privacy Compliance

When implementing data privacy and GDPR compliance:

1. **Build and Maintain a Data Inventory** — Create a comprehensive map of all personal data your system processes: what data types are collected (name, email, IP address, device ID, behavioral data), where each type is stored (databases, caches, logs, backups, third-party services), the legal basis for processing (consent, contract, legitimate interest), retention periods, and data flows between systems and third parties. Use automated data discovery tools to scan databases and storage for PII. Review and update the inventory quarterly and whenever new features or integrations are added.
2. **Implement Consent Management Correctly** — Collect consent that meets GDPR requirements: freely given (no pre-checked boxes, no service denial for refusing non-essential consent), specific (separate consent for each purpose), informed (plain language explaining what data is collected and why), and unambiguous (clear affirmative action). Store consent records with timestamp, version of privacy policy agreed to, specific purposes consented to, and the method of consent. Allow users to withdraw consent as easily as they gave it, and cease processing immediately upon withdrawal.
3. **Build Right to Erasure (Right to Be Forgotten)** — Implement a deletion pipeline that can purge a user's personal data across all systems within the 30-day GDPR deadline. This includes: primary databases, search indexes, caches, logs, backups, message queues, analytics systems, and third-party integrations. Use soft-delete with a scheduled hard-delete to handle grace periods. For data that cannot be deleted due to legal obligations (tax records, fraud prevention), document the legal basis and inform the user. Test the deletion pipeline monthly with synthetic data.
4. **Establish Data Processing Agreements (DPAs)** — Execute DPAs with every third-party processor (cloud providers, analytics tools, email services, payment processors, CDNs) before sharing personal data. DPAs must specify: processing purposes, data categories, security measures, sub-processor notification, data breach notification timelines (72 hours), audit rights, and data return/deletion on contract termination. Maintain a register of all processors and sub-processors. Review DPAs annually and when processors change their terms.
5. **Implement Privacy by Design Architecture** — Embed privacy into system architecture from the start: minimize data collection (only collect what is necessary for the stated purpose), pseudonymize data at ingestion (replace identifiers with tokens), encrypt personal data at rest and in transit, implement access controls based on data classification, design for data portability (export in machine-readable format), and build audit trails for all data access. Separate PII storage from analytics data to enable deletion without losing aggregate insights.
6. **Design Cookie Consent Flows** — Implement a cookie consent banner that: blocks all non-essential cookies until consent is given (no cookies firing on page load), categorizes cookies clearly (strictly necessary, functional, analytics, marketing), allows granular per-category consent, persists the user's choice, and provides an accessible way to modify preferences later. Use a consent management platform (CMP) that integrates with your tag manager. Test that rejecting all cookies does not break core functionality. Ensure consent is obtained before any tracking scripts load.
7. **Define and Enforce Data Retention Policies** — For each data category, define a retention period based on business need and legal requirements: transactional data (7 years for tax compliance), user profiles (duration of account plus 30 days), server logs (90 days), analytics data (anonymized after 26 months), support tickets (3 years). Implement automated deletion jobs that run daily and delete expired data. Log all deletions for audit purposes. Review retention periods annually with legal counsel.
8. **Prepare for Data Breach Notification** — Build a breach response plan that meets the 72-hour notification requirement: detection (automated alerts for unauthorized access, data exfiltration patterns, unusual query volumes), assessment (classify severity, identify affected data subjects, determine risk level), notification (notify supervisory authority within 72 hours with breach details, affected data categories, approximate number of subjects, and remediation measures), and user notification (without undue delay for high-risk breaches). Conduct breach simulation exercises bi-annually.
9. **Conduct Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs)** — Perform a DPIA before starting any processing that is likely to result in high risk to individuals: large-scale processing of sensitive data, systematic monitoring (CCTV, employee tracking), automated decision-making with legal effects, and new technology deployments. Document: the processing description, necessity and proportionality assessment, risks to data subjects, and mitigating measures. Consult your Data Protection Officer and, if residual risk remains high, consult the supervisory authority before proceeding.
10. **Handle Cross-Border Data Transfers** — Ensure lawful mechanisms for transferring personal data outside the EEA: Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) with supplementary measures (encryption, pseudonymization), adequacy decisions for approved countries, Binding Corporate Rules for intra-group transfers, or derogations for specific situations (explicit consent, contractual necessity). Conduct Transfer Impact Assessments to evaluate the legal framework in the recipient country. Document the transfer mechanism used for each data flow. Monitor for changes in adequacy decisions and legal frameworks.
11. **Implement Pseudonymization and Anonymization** — Pseudonymize personal data wherever possible: replace direct identifiers with tokens, store the mapping separately with restricted access, and use pseudonymized data for development, testing, and analytics. For true anonymization (data no longer subject to GDPR), ensure the data cannot be re-identified even with additional information — remove quasi-identifiers, apply k-anonymity or differential privacy techniques, and validate with re-identification risk assessments. Never use production PII in non-production environments.
12. **Build Comprehensive Audit Logging for Compliance** — Log all access to and operations on personal data: who accessed it (authenticated user ID), what data was accessed (table, record ID, fields), when (timestamp), why (business justification or request reference), and the outcome (success/failure). Store audit logs in a tamper-evident system separate from application databases. Retain audit logs for the longer of your retention policy or regulatory requirement (typically 3-7 years). Enable audit log queries for DSAR (Data Subject Access Request) fulfillment and regulatory investigations.
13. **Implement Data Subject Access Requests (DSARs)** — Build a self-service portal or automated pipeline for handling DSARs within the 30-day deadline: identity verification (multi-factor, not just email), data collection from all systems in the data inventory, compilation into a machine-readable format (JSON, CSV), review for third-party data that should be redacted, and secure delivery to the data subject. Track DSAR volume, processing time, and completion rate. Automate as much as possible to handle volume spikes without missing deadlines.
14. **Train Engineering Teams on Privacy Requirements** — Conduct mandatory privacy training for all engineers annually covering: GDPR principles and rights, privacy by design patterns, secure data handling practices, breach recognition and reporting, and how to escalate privacy concerns. Include privacy review checkpoints in the development process: design review (DPIA trigger check), code review (PII handling patterns), and deployment review (data flow validation). Make privacy a first-class engineering concern, not just a legal checkbox.
